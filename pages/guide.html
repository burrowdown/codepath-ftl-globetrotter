Ways to Access the Canyon

Multi-day rafting trips on the Snake River are the classic way to experience Hells Canyon. The river corridor is only accessible by water or trail through much of the wilderness, so a rafting trip doubles as your gateway to remote campsites, petroglyphs, and wildlife. Most outfitters run 3- to 6-day trips from late May through September. The 214-mile Hells Canyon Scenic Byway loops through northeastern Oregon, offering overlooks, small towns, and trailheads without requiring a boat or long backcountry hike. Key stops include the Hells Canyon Dam Overlook, Hat Point (elevation 6,982 feet), and the historic Imnaha community along the river of the same name. Over 900 miles of trails wind through Hells Canyon National Recreation Area, ranging from short day hikes to multi-week wilderness routes. The Snake River Trail follows the river for 24 miles through the inner gorge, while the Seven Devils Loop takes backpackers through alpine lakes and granite peaks high above the canyon floor. Commercial jet boat tours are the most accessible way to reach the inner canyon without a multi-day commitment. Departing from Lewiston, Idaho or the Hells Canyon Dam boat launch, tours cover stretches of the river that are otherwise reachable only by raft or foot. Most operators offer half-day and full-day trips, with stops at historic ranches, Native American pictographs, and wildlife viewing areas along the way. Experienced boaters with their own jet boats can launch at Hells Canyon Dam and travel upriver into the wilderness at their own pace. A free permit is required for overnight trips, and boaters must be prepared for Class IV rapids, rapidly changing water levels, and sections with no cell service or emergency access. It's the most flexible way to experience the canyon, and the most demanding. Several outfitters offer guided horseback trips into the Hells Canyon Wilderness, ranging from day rides along the rim to multi-day pack trips into the inner canyon. Horses and mules can access terrain that's impractical on foot, covering long distances across open ridgelines and through forested drainages. It remains one of the most traditional — and unhurried — ways to take in the canyon.
